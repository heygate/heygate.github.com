Press Archive

Below is a small selection of articles about the Heygate. The numerous references to sink estates and crime served well to reinforce the council's and developer's regeneration agenda, and erase the real history of the estate and its residents. None of the articles mention the dis-investment by the Council, which accounted for a lot of the bad conditions in the infrastructure of The Heygate. Such dis-investment over time was always useful for the Council in giving residents very little real choice about regeneration possibilities. Many of the press articles suggest that the 70′s architecture itself created poverty and deprivation, without taking into account the council's local housing policies - i.e. its decision to stop issuing secure tenancies in the late 1990s and use of housing allocation for its temporary housing obligations. Despite these policies, which undoubtedly had a negative effect on deprivation indicators, Met Police statistics show a crime rate on the estate that was 45% below the borough average.

    • To what extent the media coverage has been affected by general prejudices towards modernist architecture and to what extent by council and developer press releases remains open to question. Councillor Colley once described it as a 'concrete fortress' and a 'blight on the landscape'. Council leader Peter John described the estate as "a by-word for social failure, for crime and anti-social behaviour".
    Subsequent press articles saw the estate labelled "Muggers' paradise" by the BBC and the Evening Standard, and a "human dustbin" where people "live like pigs" in the Daily Telegraph.



    Filming has always been a bone of contention on the estate; ever since the first time it was used as an urban crime backdrop in the 1989 Denzel Washington film Queen & Country. The council has always argued that it ensures the films don't use the estate's name and that it provides much-needed income for the council. It was only after years of complaints from residents that films portraying drugs and violence were banned from using the estate in 2012. It was recently disclosed that Southwark outsources its film location services, and that around half of its annual revenue from filming in Southwark is paid to its outsourcing company filmfixer.co.uk.
